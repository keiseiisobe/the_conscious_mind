# Does IIT's axiom of intrinsic existence conflict with Dennett's denial of consciousness?

## Short Answer

**Yes — it is a direct, foundational conflict.** IIT treats phenomenal experience as the one undeniable starting point and builds its entire framework from it. Dennett denies that phenomenal experience — in the philosophically robust sense IIT requires — is real at all.

---

## The Conflict

### IIT's Axiom: Intrinsic Existence

IIT begins from a Cartesian premise: my own experience is the one thing whose existence is immediately and absolutely certain. It exists *for me*, from my own intrinsic perspective. This is not a hypothesis to be tested — it is the axiom from which everything else follows.

The entire methodology of IIT depends on this: reason *from* phenomenology *to* physics. The physical substrate of consciousness must be inferred from what experience irreducibly is.

### Dennett's Position

Dennett does not say there is *nothing* happening in the brain. He says there are no **qualia** in the philosophically loaded sense IIT presupposes. Key claims from *Consciousness Explained* (1991):

- What we call "experience" is the brain constructing narratives about its own processing
- There is no "Cartesian theater" where experience actually *appears* to a subject
- The felt quality you feel certain of is a **user illusion** — like a desktop GUI that misrepresents the underlying computation
- Introspection is not a reliable window into real phenomenal states; it is itself a representational process that can be systematically wrong

His method — **heterophenomenology** — treats first-person reports as data about what the brain *represents*, not as evidence of genuine phenomenal reality.

---

## Why It Is Irreconcilable

| | IIT | Dennett |
|---|---|---|
| **Starting point** | Experience is the most certain thing that exists | Experience-as-felt is a user illusion |
| **Method** | Reason from phenomenology to physics | Explain phenomenology away via functional mechanisms |
| **Qualia** | Real, foundational, the explanandum | Not real in the metaphysically robust sense |
| **Axiom 1 status** | Self-evident truth | Question-begging assumption |

---

## The Mutual Objections

**Dennett against IIT:** IIT is **question-begging**. It assumes the thing that needs to be explained — that phenomenal experience is real and irreducible — and then constructs an elaborate mathematical theory on that assumption. If the foundation is an illusion, the whole edifice collapses.

**IIT against Dennett:** Dennett is denying the one thing that *cannot* be denied. The existence of experience is more certain than any physical fact. To call it an illusion is self-undermining — an illusion still requires something it is like to *seem* a certain way. The explanatory gap doesn't close; it relocates.

> If it seems like something, then something is seeming — and that seeming still demands explanation.

---

## Connection to the Broader Debate

This conflict mirrors the broader fault line between **illusionists** (Dennett, Frankish) and **phenomenal realists** (Chalmers, Tononi). See also: [Should illusionists prove how material can cause illusion?](do-illusionists-need-to-prove-how-matter-causes-illusion.md)

Chalmers formalized the pressure on Dennett's side as the **meta-problem of consciousness**: even if you deny phenomenal consciousness, you must explain *why we so strongly believe we have it* — and that problem is nearly as hard as the original.
