# IIT Axioms

## Overview

Integrated Information Theory (IIT) starts not from the brain, but from **phenomenal experience itself**. It identifies five properties that are self-evidently true of *every possible experience*, then derives corresponding physical requirements for whatever substrate gives rise to that experience.

> "IIT starts from the essential phenomenal properties of experience, or axioms, and infers postulates about the characteristics that are required of its physical substrate."
> — Tononi, Boly, Massimini & Koch (2016)

Each axiom describes a feature of experience. Each has a paired **postulate** — a constraint on the physical substrate of consciousness (PSC).

---

## The Five Axioms

### 1. Intrinsic Existence

**Experience exists intrinsically.**

My own experience is the only thing whose existence is immediately and absolutely evident. It exists *for me*, from my own intrinsic perspective — not from the outside.

> As Descartes recognized: my experience is the one thing I cannot doubt.

| Axiom | Postulate |
|---|---|
| Experience exists for the subject, intrinsically | The PSC must have **cause–effect power for itself** — it must be able to affect and be affected by its own parts, from its own intrinsic perspective |

**Physical implication:** A neuron satisfies existence because it has states that can be caused and can cause others. A system of two reciprocally connected neurons satisfies *intrinsic* existence because they make a difference to each other.

---

### 2. Composition

**Experience is structured — composed of multiple phenomenal distinctions that coexist within it.**

Within a single experience, I may simultaneously distinguish a piano, a blue color, a book, spatial locations, etc. These distinctions are not separate experiences; they compose one structured whole.

| Axiom | Postulate |
|---|---|
| Experience is composed of multiple coexisting distinctions | The elements of the PSC must each have **cause–effect power within the PSC**, individually (first-order mechanisms) and in combination (higher-order mechanisms) |

```
One experience can contain:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  [blue]  [book]  [left side]  [piano]   │
  │      all coexist in a single moment      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

### 3. Information

**Experience is specific — it is composed of a particular set of distinctions that make it *this* experience and not any other.**

The current experience includes seeing a *blue* book (not a red one), hands *on* the piano (not off it). The specificity of experience is what gives it its unique character.

| Axiom | Postulate |
|---|---|
| Experience is specific, made of particular distinctions | The PSC must specify a **cause–effect structure of a specific form** — a particular set of cause–effect repertoires that distinguish it from all other possible states |

A **cause–effect repertoire** specifies how a mechanism in its current state constrains the probability distribution of past and future states of the system. The full set of these repertoires across all mechanisms is the **cause–effect structure**.

---

### 4. Integration

**Experience is unitary — its distinctions are bound together irreducibly into one whole.**

I experience a complete visual scene. It cannot be split into two independent experiences (e.g., left visual field + right visual field experienced separately). The whole is irreducible to its parts.

| Axiom | Postulate |
|---|---|
| Experience cannot be subdivided into independent parts | The PSC's cause–effect structure must be **irreducible** — it cannot be decomposed into non-interdependent subsystems |

**Phi (Φ):** The degree of irreducibility is measured as **integrated information** — the minimum distance between the intact cause–effect structure and any partitioned version of it.

```
High Φ  →  strongly integrated  →  more unified experience
Low Φ   →  weakly integrated   →  experience approaches zero
Φ = 0   →  no integration      →  no experience
```

Integration requires bidirectional interactions: every part must have both causes *and* effects within the rest of the system.

---

### 5. Exclusion

**Experience is definite — it has a specific content and a specific spatio-temporal grain, neither more nor less.**

My current experience includes hands on a piano and a blue book — not a version with less content (no color) or more content (feeling blood pressure). The duration of each moment of consciousness is also definite (tens to hundreds of milliseconds), not microseconds or minutes.

| Axiom | Postulate |
|---|---|
| Experience has definite content and grain | The PSC must specify a **maximally irreducible** cause–effect structure (Φ^max) over a definite set of elements at a definite spatio-temporal grain — no more, no less |

This maximum is called a **conceptual structure**, and the system that achieves it is called a **complex**. Any overlapping cause–effect structure over the same elements is excluded — only one experience can exist at a given place and time.

---

## Summary Table

| # | Axiom | Property of Experience | Key Measure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Intrinsic Existence** | Experience exists for the subject | Cause–effect power (intrinsic) |
| 2 | **Composition** | Experience has internal structure | Mechanisms (1st- and higher-order) |
| 3 | **Information** | Experience is specific | Cause–effect repertoire |
| 4 | **Integration** | Experience is unified and irreducible | Φ (integrated information) |
| 5 | **Exclusion** | Experience is definite in content and grain | Φ^max (conceptual structure) |

---

## From Axioms to Physics

```
Axiom (phenomenology)         Postulate (physics)
──────────────────────        ──────────────────────────────────
Intrinsic existence      →    Intrinsic cause–effect power
Composition              →    Elements have cause–effect power
Information              →    Specific cause–effect structure
Integration              →    Irreducible cause–effect structure (Φ)
Exclusion                →    Maximally irreducible structure (Φ^max)
```

A physical system that satisfies all five postulates constitutes a **complex** — the physical substrate of consciousness. Its conceptual structure is identical to the experience itself.
