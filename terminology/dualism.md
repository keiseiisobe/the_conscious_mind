# Dualism

## General Definition

Dualism is the view that **reality consists of two fundamentally different kinds of things** that cannot be reduced to one another.

---

## The Classic Version: Descartes (Mind-Body Dualism)

René Descartes (17th century) argued there are two distinct substances:

| Mind (*res cogitans*) | Body (*res extensa*) |
|---|---|
| Thinking, unextended | Physical, extended in space |
| Subjective, private | Objective, measurable |
| Cannot be divided | Has spatial parts |

**The interaction problem**: If they're totally separate, *how do they affect each other?* (e.g., deciding to raise your arm actually raises it)

---

## Major Variants

| Type | Core Claim |
|---|---|
| **Substance dualism** | Mind and body are two *different substances* (Descartes) |
| **Property dualism** | One substance (physical), but it has two *irreducible kinds of properties* — physical and mental |
| **Predicate dualism** | Mental and physical *descriptions* are irreducible, even if the underlying reality is one |

---

## The Opposite View

**Monism** — everything is one kind of thing:
- **Physicalism/Materialism**: only matter exists; mind reduces to brain
- **Idealism**: only mind exists; matter reduces to experience

---

## Dualism in *The Conscious Mind*

Chalmers adopts **property dualism** — the brain is physical, but **conscious experience** is an additional, irreducible property that physics alone cannot capture.

From the preface:

> "I argue that reductive explanation of consciousness is impossible, and I even argue for a form of dualism. But this is just part of the scientific process... Everything I say here is compatible with the results of contemporary science; our picture of the natural world is broadened, not overturned."

### Key points of Chalmers' dualism

- ✅ **Compatible with science** — he's not rejecting neuroscience or physics
- ✅ **Naturalistic** — consciousness is still *part of nature*, not supernatural
- ❌ **Not substance dualism** — he does not posit a separate non-physical soul

Even if we mapped every neuron in the brain perfectly, we still couldn't answer *"but why does it **feel** like something?"* — that explanatory gap is what forces Chalmers toward dualism, and sets up his central concept: the **hard problem of consciousness**.
